The Work

The
work

In a 2011 New Yorker article, surgeon Atul Gawande described how even expert performers plateau without someone outside their own head to show them what they cannot see. Years into a distinguished career, a coach watching him operate spotted habits he had no idea he had developed. The external perspective produced improvements he couldn’t have generated alone.

The same is true of leadership — and the more capable the leader, the more this matters. Expertise and self-awareness are not the same thing. We rarely see ourselves as others do. And even when we know what we should do differently, doing it is another matter.

How an engagement works

An engagement is ongoing, confidential, and built around the leader’s real work.

Initial conversation. The work begins with a conversation about the leader, the context, and the questions that matter most.

Confidential interviews. Michael speaks with the right people to understand how the leader is experienced, what is working well, and where improvement would matter.

Regular one-on-one work. Conversations are more frequent at the beginning, then typically every two weeks, depending on the leader’s schedule.

Real-time counsel. Michael is available when something important arises: a difficult decision, a relationship issue, a board moment, or a conversation that needs preparation.

Independent observation. Where useful, he observes leadership meetings, board meetings, or critical presentations — not as a participant, but as an informed advisor.

Six-month commitment. Engagements begin with a six-month commitment and are structured as a fixed-fee arrangement. For pricing and availability, contact Michael directly.

Confidentiality is sacred: the content of conversations does not leave the relationship, and Michael does not report to boards, investors, or organizations about what a leader shares. The relationship is built on that trust.

What changes

Leaders who do this work make better decisions — not occasionally, but as a pattern. They become more honest about what is and isn’t working, more confident in their own strengths, and more effective in the relationships that matter most: the board, the executive team, the key hire. The problems that once created anxiety become problems they know how to navigate. That is the outcome: a leader who is clearer, calmer, and more capable — not in theory, but in the moments that count.

Research

The research
behind the work

Michael’s doctoral research at Harvard examined leadership effectiveness in biotech R&D — an environment defined by scientific complexity, high stakes, and sustained uncertainty. What he found was counterintuitive: even excellent leaders undermine themselves. Not through incompetence, but through predictable behavioral patterns.

The research showed how leaders in stressful situations make decisions differently than they do under ordinary conditions. Important data go unexamined. Emotions become hot rather than informative. Attention narrows rather than widens. Self-regulation and interpersonal skills seem to vanish. Strengths become blind spots.

The good news is that these patterns are predictable, even if standard leadership coaching often fails to account for them. Fifty years of behavioral science research — spanning behavioral economics, decision-making, psychology, and social psychology — does more than describe these patterns. It explains the mechanisms behind them and shows how they can be interrupted.

Michael applies that research directly. Rather than imposing a generic framework on a leader’s situation, he uses the research to help leaders recognize what is happening, understand why it is happening, and create the conditions for a different outcome.

He teaches the psychology of decision-making at Brown University and has previously taught at Harvard and MIT. He served as Professor of Leadership and Ethics at the U.S. Naval War College. His coaching draws on that academic work — and on his own experience as an operator and investor who has seen these patterns up close.

Even excellent leaders undermine themselves, if rarely. The patterns are predictable — and that predictability is what makes them addressable.

Michael Shiner is a Boston-based executive coach who works with founders, CEOs, and senior leaders in health care and life sciences.

He began his career as an entrepreneur — building and selling a company before becoming a family-office investor in mid-market companies in Canada. Working closely with senior leaders across those years, he saw how leadership pressures grow as organizations grow, and how often the most consequential leadership problems remain invisible until they have already shaped decisions, relationships, and trust.

That experience drew him to doctoral research at Harvard, where he studied leadership effectiveness and psychology in biotech R&D. His research found that even excellent leaders undermine themselves through predictable behavioral patterns — patterns that behavioral science can explain and, with the right support, interrupt. He brought that research directly into his work as a coach.

He teaches the psychology of decision-making at Brown University and has also taught at Harvard and MIT. He served as Professor of Leadership and Ethics at the U.S. Naval War College. He is co-authoring a book with Jonathan Shay, M.D., Ph.D., on destructive leadership and its consequences.

Michael works with a small number of clients at a time. He is often introduced by investors, board members, CEOs, CHROs, and is glad to hear from leaders directly. He lives in Newton, MA.
