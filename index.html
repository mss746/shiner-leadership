Shiner Leadership

Even the best leaders have blind spots. The right coach helps them see what they can’t.

Michael Shiner is a Boston-based executive coach who works with a small number of next-generation health care and life-sciences leaders.

He works confidentially with founders, CEOs, and senior leaders.

Investors, board members, CEOs, and CHROs introduce Michael at moments when a leader’s responsibilities have begun to change:

  • During and after fundraising rounds
  • As founders become CEOs
  • As high-performing leaders move into broader enterprise roles, including the C-suite
  • When experienced CEOs and senior leaders seek coaching as a matter of good leadership practice, or during a setback or transition

Michael has worked with leaders at Biogen, Vertex, CVS, and Massachusetts General Hospital and more broadly across technology, professional services, and defense — including Microsoft, Amazon, Deloitte, Ernst & Young, and the U.S. Navy.

The Work

The
work

In a 2011 New Yorker article, surgeon Atul Gawande described how even expert performers plateau without someone outside their own head to show them what they cannot see. Years into a distinguished career, a coach watching him operate spotted habits he had no idea he had developed. The external perspective produced improvements he couldn’t have generated alone.

The same is true of leadership — and the more capable the leader, the more this matters. Expertise and self-awareness are not the same thing. We rarely see ourselves as others do. And even when we know what we should do differently, doing it is another matter.

How an engagement works

An engagement begins with a conversation. Michael conducts confidential interviews with the right people to develop an early picture of how the leader is experienced, what is working well, and where the leader can improve. That interview data is an input into what the leader chooses to work on and how.

The engagement itself is ongoing and confidential. It is built around regular one-on-one conversations — typically every two to three weeks — combined with real-time availability when something important arises: a decision that needs a sounding board, a difficult conversation that requires preparation, a relationship that has become complicated, or a moment of uncertainty where candid counsel matters. Over time, Michael develops a detailed understanding of the leader, the company, the team dynamics, and the pressures shaping decisions. Where useful, he observes leadership meetings, board meetings, or critical presentations — not as a participant, but as an informed and independent advisor.

Engagements begin with a six-month commitment. Michael works with a small number of leaders at any given time — by design, not convenience. Confidentiality is sacred: the content of conversations does not leave the relationship, and Michael does not report to boards, investors, or organizations about what a leader shares. The relationship is built on that trust.

Engagements are structured as a fixed-fee commitment, front-loaded to reflect the intensity of early work. For pricing and availability, contact Michael directly.

What changes

Leaders who do this work make better decisions — not occasionally, but as a pattern. They become more precise about what they actually think, more honest about what is and isn’t working, and more effective in the relationships that matter most: the board, the executive team, the key hire they’re trying to keep. The problems that once created anxiety become problems they know how to navigate. That is the outcome: a leader who is clearer, calmer, and more capable — not in theory, but in the moments that count.

Research

The research
behind the work

Michael’s doctoral research at Harvard examined leadership effectiveness in biotech R&D — an environment defined by scientific complexity, high stakes, and sustained uncertainty. What he found was counterintuitive: even excellent leaders undermine themselves. Not through incompetence, but through predictable behavioral patterns.

The research showed how leaders in stressful situations make decisions differently than they do under ordinary conditions. Important data go unexamined. Emotions become hot rather than informative. Attention narrows rather than widens. Self-regulaton and interpersonal skills seem to vanish. Strengths become blind spots.

The good news is that these patterns are predictable, even if standard leadership coaching often fails to account for them. Fifty years of behavioral science research — spanning behavioral economics, decision-making, psychology, and social psychology — does more than describe these patterns. It explains the mechanisms behind them and shows how they can be interrupted.

Michael applies that research directly. Rather than imposing a generic framework on a leader’s situation, he uses the research to help leaders recognize what is happening, understand why it is happening, and create the conditions for a different outcome.

He teaches the psychology of decision-making at Brown University and has previously taught at Harvard and MIT. He served as Professor of Leadership and Ethics at the U.S. Naval War College. His coaching draws on that academic work — and on his own experience as an operator and investor who has seen these patterns up close.

Even excellent leaders undermine themselves, if rarely. The patterns are predictable — and that predictability is what makes them addressable.
